\section{Supplementary Figures --- v6 Model}
\label{sec:appendix}

This appendix documents the current (v6) checkpoint only. It focuses on the
two questions the project exists to answer: (1) does the diffusion-generated
ensemble match the true physics of 2D compact U(1) lattice gauge theory, at
couplings inside and outside what the model trained on, and (2) how does
producing that ensemble compare, in cost, to standard HMC --- both in how
fast a usable configuration appears, and in what standard HMC's own
``equilibrium'' ensemble actually contains at the same coupling.

\subsection{Methodology}
\label{sec:appendix-methods}

\paragraph{The model.} A score network is trained to reverse a noising
process applied to wrapped link angles $\theta \in (-\pi, \pi]$, conditioned
on a \emph{coarse} (2$\times$2-blocked) version of the same field.
Generation runs that reverse process backward from noise, which is exactly
one inverse renormalization group step: it lifts a coarse-lattice
configuration, produced by ordinary HMC at a coarse coupling $\beta_c$, up
to a fine-lattice configuration at a target coupling $\beta_f$ (tree-level
relation $\beta_c = \beta_f / 4$). Two mechanisms make this work outside the
training set:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Exact-score blend at small noise.} As the reverse process
    approaches zero noise, the learned score is blended toward the
    \emph{analytically exact} score of the (Gaussian-smeared) Wilson
    action. Near $\sigma = 0$ this makes the last steps of generation
    literal Langevin dynamics under the true action --- correct by
    construction, regardless of what the network learned from data. This is
    the main reason the model can be asked for couplings the training set
    never covered: the fine-detail correctness at small noise comes from
    the known physics of the action, not from interpolating training
    examples.
  \item \textbf{Structural topology transport.} The coarse ensemble's
    topological charge $Q_c$ is known exactly (it is an integer, computed
    directly from the coarse configuration) and is deterministically
    projected onto the generated fine configuration, rather than left to
    emerge from the sampling dynamics. This matters because standard HMC
    \emph{cannot} change $Q$ reliably at large $\beta$
    (Section~\ref{sec:appendix-therm}) --- by not relying on stochastic
    tunneling for its topological content, the pipeline sidesteps HMC's
    worst failure mode entirely.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Training coverage, precisely.} Training couplings are drawn
continuously, log-uniform in $\beta$ (\texttt{data.random\_rungs}, see
\texttt{diffusion.utils.expand\_rungs}), not from a fixed discrete list: 64
rungs at $L=16$ spanning $\beta \in [1.0, 57.2]$, 12 at $L=32$ spanning
$\beta \in [2.2, 52.6]$, 6 at $L=8$ spanning $\beta \in [1.4, 2.5]$, plus 4
fixed high-statistics anchors at $L=16$ ($\beta = 14.15, 25, 40, 55.02$).
Inside $\beta \approx [1, 60]$, any requested coupling sits close to some
training draw --- this is \textbf{in-sample} generalization (interpolation).
$\beta > 60$ is where training coverage stops entirely --- this is
\textbf{out-of-sample} generalization (extrapolation). Lattice sizes $L=64$
and $L=128$ were never trained on at any coupling --- a separate, orthogonal
out-of-sample axis (volume).

\paragraph{How a figure is derived.} Every distribution-match figure is
produced by \texttt{diffusion.validate.report.validate\_ensemble}: it
measures plaquette angles, Wilson loops (areas 1 to 144), and topological
charge on the generated batch, compares each to the \textbf{exact}
closed-form prediction (2D compact U(1) admits an exact character-expansion
solution for the plaquette distribution, Wilson loop expectations, and the
finite-volume $P(Q)$ --- \texttt{diffusion.lgt.exact} --- no Monte Carlo
involved on the ``exact'' side at all), and reports a $z$-score
$z = (\text{measured} - \text{exact})\,/\,(\text{statistical error of the
measurement})$. $|z| \lesssim 2$ is a pass. A reference HMC ensemble is also
shown for visual comparison; \emph{which} HMC reference matters and is
stated per figure --- an unbiased reference with instanton ($Q$-hop) updates
for ``does the model match the physics'' figures
(Section~\ref{sec:appendix-gen}), versus the case's own plain HMC with no
topological updates for ``what does standard practice actually produce''
figures (Section~\ref{sec:appendix-therm}), since only the latter can
honestly demonstrate a failure mode of standard HMC.

\subsection{Thermalization: cost vs. standard HMC, and why standard HMC struggles}
\label{sec:appendix-therm}
% ===================================================================

\paragraph{The physics of why standard HMC fails at large $\beta$.} Two
distinct mechanisms, both worsening with $\beta$:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Critical slowing down} --- the integrated autocorrelation
    time of ordinary (non-topological) observables like the plaquette or
    small Wilson loops grows with $\beta$, so a chain needs more
    trajectories between independent samples even once it has reached
    equilibrium.
  \item \textbf{Topological freezing} --- the topological charge $Q$
    changes only via a global rearrangement of the field (an
    instanton-like tunneling event). The rate of these events is
    suppressed roughly as $e^{-2\beta}$ with the action barrier between
    sectors, so above a fairly modest coupling a standard HMC chain's $Q$
    simply stops moving for any practical trajectory budget. This is
    qualitatively different from (1): it isn't slow mixing, it's zero
    mixing --- the chain is stuck in whatever sector it happened to be in,
    forever, as far as any feasible run is concerned.
\end{enumerate}

Standard HMC also has \textbf{two distinct costs}, and figures below are
careful to label which one is meant: \textbf{burn-in} (the one-time cost of
reaching equilibrium from a fresh hot or cold start) and the \textbf{sampling
interval} $2\tau_{\mathrm{int}}$ (once \emph{already} equilibrated, the
trajectories a chain must run and discard before its \emph{next}
configuration is a statistically independent draw --- a different,
perpetual cost). All thermalization figures use the case's own \textbf{plain
HMC, no topological updates} as the standard-practice comparison --- the
instanton move used to build unbiased references in
Section~\ref{sec:appendix-gen} is this pipeline's own fix and is
deliberately excluded here.

\subsubsection{Why standard HMC's equilibrium ensemble is wrong, not just slow}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/09_freezing_zscore.png}
  \caption{\textbf{$\langle Q^2\rangle$ $z$-score vs.\ $\beta_f$: the
    pipeline vs.\ two independent plain-HMC ensembles.} Ensembles built the
    way a practitioner actually would --- one $Q$ sample per independent
    chain, taken after burn-in (repeat samples from a frozen chain carry no
    new information, so this is the correct unit of evidence, not a trick).
    The pipeline (blue) stays inside $|z| \lesssim 2$ across the full
    three-decade range. The hot-start ensemble crosses out of tolerance by
    $\beta_f \approx 6$ and settles at $z \approx 5$--$7$ (a stable bias:
    each frozen chain lands in a different wrong sector, so the ensemble
    mean is systematically off). The cold-start ensemble is worse in a
    different way --- above $\beta_f \approx 9$, essentially every chain
    freezes at exactly $Q=0$, giving zero measured variance and a formally
    enormous $z$ (the honest statement is ``no measured topological
    fluctuation at all,'' not merely ``biased''). \textbf{Significance:}
    demonstrates that the failure above is not a speed problem that more
    trajectories would fix --- it's structural. No feasible trajectory
    budget changes this outcome for standard HMC.}
  \label{fig:freezing-zscore}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/10_freezing_pq.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Full $P(Q)$ shape at three couplings spanning ergodic

    fraction of any standard-HMC cost measured in
    Section~\ref{sec:appendix-therm}) takes a seed with a real residual
    Wilson-loop bias to comfortably passing, while a fresh hot- or
    cold-start chain never reaches tolerance within the 640-trajectory
    budget at all --- the clearest single illustration that this is not
    ``faster,'' it is ``standard HMC does not arrive at all within any
    practical budget, and the diffusion route does.''}
  \label{fig:high-after}
\end{figure}

% ===================================================================
\subsection{Autocorrelation: fast vs.\ slow modes, over a window of separations}
\label{sec:appendix-autocorr}
% ===================================================================

Everywhere above, ``thermalization'' and ``tunneling'' were described in
terms of a single summary number ($t_{\mathrm{therm}}$, $\tau_{\mathrm{int}}$,
tunneling counts). This section shows the underlying curve those numbers are
extracted from: the normalized autocorrelation function $\Gamma(\delta)$ of
an observable as a function of separation $\delta$ (in HMC trajectories),
computed on the equilibrated window of each chain (post-$t_{\mathrm{therm}}$,
or the second half of the run for a start that never thermalizes), averaged
over chains with SEM error bars. $\Gamma(0) = 1$ by construction; a
well-mixing chain decays toward $\Gamma(\delta) \approx 0$ as $\delta$ grows,
and a flat $\Gamma(\delta)$ that never decays is the direct signature of a
mode the chain cannot move at all within the window tested. \textbf{How
derived:} both figures below reuse
\texttt{diffusion.validate.stats.normalized\_autocorrelation} and the
per-trajectory series already saved by the thermalization benchmark
(\texttt{\{label\}\_series.npz}) --- no new HMC was run for this section,
only new plots (\texttt{diffusion/scripts/11\_autocorrelation.py}) over data
already used elsewhere in this appendix (same three cases as
Figures~\ref{fig:low-relax}--\ref{fig:high-after}: $\beta_f = 1.49$, $55.0$,
and $218.6/L{=}64$).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.95\textwidth]{figures/20_autocorr_modes.png}
  \caption{\textbf{Fast modes (Wilson loops, blue) vs.\ slow modes
    (topology, red) side by side, for all three starts (rows) and all three
    couplings (columns).} \textbf{Physics:} the plaquette is a local,
    fast-mixing observable --- it only requires a few nearby link angles to
    change to decorrelate --- while the topological charge is a genuinely
    global mode: changing it requires an instanton-like rearrangement of
    the whole field, which plain HMC (no $Q$-hops) simply does not do at
    appreciable coupling. \textbf{What the panels show:} at $\beta_f = 1.49$
    (left column) both modes decorrelate within about 5 trajectories for
    every start --- weak coupling, nothing frozen. At $\beta_f = 55.0$ and
    $218.6$ (center, right columns), the blue (plaquette) curves still
    decay toward zero for every start, just more slowly at higher $\beta$
    --- ordinary critical slowing down. The red ($Q$) curves, by contrast,
    sit pinned at $\Gamma(\delta) = 1.000$ out to the full 30-trajectory
    window tested, for \textbf{every} start including the diffusion seed's
    own post-generation continuation. \textbf{Significance:} this is not a
    defect in the diffusion route --- once generation is done, the seed is
    handed to the same plain-HMC continuation used everywhere in this
    appendix (no $Q$-hops), so of course its topological charge cannot move
    during that continuation either; nothing here relies on it moving. The
    correctness demonstrated in Section~\ref{sec:appendix-gen} and
    Figures~\ref{fig:freezing-zscore}--\ref{fig:freezing-pq} comes from the
    sector being set correctly at generation time (structural transport,
    Section~\ref{sec:appendix-methods}), not from letting it mix
    afterward. What this figure adds is the direct visual proof that the
    flat-red/decaying-blue pattern is a real, measured property of the
    dynamics --- not an inference from a single $\tau_{\mathrm{int}}$
    number --- and that it is identical in kind for all three starts:
    plain HMC's topological mode simply does not move at these couplings,
    regardless of where the chain began.}

\end{figure}
